# Spanish (Mexico) — voice guide

The Spanish-speaking population in San Mateo County is heavily Mexican-origin. The translation must read as Mexican Spanish — not Castilian, not Caribbean, not "neutral Latin American".

## Address form

- **Use `tú`, never `usted`.** This is a small family business addressing neighbors. `usted` reads as cold or stiffly formal.
- Imperatives: "Llámanos" (not "Llámenos"). "Pídenos" (not "Pídanos").

## Tone

Warm, direct, neighborly. Mexican Spanish in California — confident but never aggressive. Avoid excessive diminutives ("-ito" / "-ita") that read as caricature.

## Vocabulary

- "Pickup" → use the brand name verbatim where it appears as a noun ("Peninsula Pick Ups"). When referring to the service, prefer "recolección" or "acarreo".
- "Junk removal" → "recolección de chatarra" or "retiro de basura grande". Avoid "remoción" — sounds clinical.
- "Free quote" → "cotización gratis" (not "presupuesto gratuito" — too European).
- "Same-day service" → "servicio el mismo día".
- "Hauling" → "acarreo".
- "Cleanout" → "limpieza completa" or "vaciado".
- "Truck" → "troca" reads as warmly Mexican-American but may be too colloquial for headers; use "camión" in formal copy and reserve "troca" for body text where the warmth matters.

## What to avoid

- **Castilian markers**: "vosotros", "ordenador", "móvil", "vale" (use "celular", "está bien", "carro", etc.).
- **Caribbean markers**: dropping final -s, "guagua".
- **Spain-specific business words**: "presupuesto", "currar", "guay".
- **Direct calques from English**: "no problema" (use "no hay problema"), "te llamo de vuelta" (use "te regreso la llamada").

## Trust phrases (locked translations)

- "Licensed and insured" → "Con licencia y seguro"
- "Family owned" → "Negocio familiar"
- "Locally owned" → "Negocio local"
- "Verified business line" → "Línea verificada del negocio"
- "Free quote" → "Cotización gratis"
