# Portuguese (Brazil) — voice guide

The Portuguese-speaking community in San Mateo County is overwhelmingly Brazilian (1980s-onward immigration plus subsequent family migration). Translation should read as **Brazilian Portuguese**, not Continental (European) Portuguese — those are two distinctly different registers and grammars.

## Tone

Warm, direct, neighborly. Brazilian Portuguese has a relatively informal baseline for service-business communication compared to European Portuguese. The voice should be confident and approachable — Don and Melissa as your friendly local hauler, not a faceless corporation.

## Address form

- Use **você** (informal-but-respectful "you") consistently. This is the Brazilian standard; using **tu** (with the matching verb forms) reads as European Portuguese or Southern-Brazilian regional, not pan-Brazilian.
- Refer to the business as **nós** (we) or implied subject. Direct: "Nós cuidamos da remoção" / "Cuidamos da remoção."
- Imperatives are direct: **ligue** (call), **peça** (request), **agende** (schedule). No softening required for service-business CTAs.

## Vocabulary

- **"Junk removal"** → **remoção de entulho** is the standard service term (entulho covers debris/junk broadly). **coleta de lixo** specifically means trash collection (municipal-style) — not what we want. Use remoção de entulho.
- **"Hauling"** → **transporte** (transport) is general; **frete** (freight/haulage) is closer to the trade term.
- **"Cleanout"** → **limpeza completa** (full cleaning) for whole-property; **desocupação** for clearing out a unit.
- **"Pickup"** as a service action → **retirada** (pickup/collection). Keep "Peninsula Pick Ups" verbatim.
- **"Free quote"** → **orçamento gratuito** (free estimate). Standard.
- **"Same-day service"** → **atendimento no mesmo dia** (service the same day).
- **"Family-owned"** → **empresa familiar** (family business). Warm, clear.
- **"Licensed and insured"** → **licenciados e segurados** (licensed and insured — past participles agreeing with the implied "we" subject).

## What to avoid

- **European Portuguese vocabulary**: avoid "comboio" (use ônibus / ferramenta-specific Brazilian forms instead), "empresa de mudanças" specifically European, "pequeno-almoço," "casa de banho" — these mark the writer as European, not Brazilian.
- **European Portuguese verb conjugation**: "tu vais" (EU) vs "você vai" (BR). Always BR.
- **European Portuguese spelling reform issues**: post-2009 spelling reform mostly aligned the variants, but watch for residual "facto" (EU) vs "fato" (BR), "óptimo" (EU) vs "ótimo" (BR).
- **Aggressive sales language** — even direct, Brazilian readers are skeptical of pushy marketing. Confidence beats hyperbole. "O preço não muda" beats "INACREDITÁVEL OFERTA!"
- **Cariocas-only slang** — keep the register pan-Brazilian. "Bacana" is fine; "irado" is too São-Paulo-specific.

## Trust phrases (locked translations)

- "Licensed and insured" → Licenciados e segurados
- "Family owned" → Empresa familiar
- "Locally owned" → Empresa local
- "Verified business line" → Linha comercial verificada
- "Free quote" → Orçamento gratuito

## Phone numbers and brand names

- Phone number `(650) 201-1543` renders LTR exactly as `(650) 201-1543`. Never reformatted to Brazilian conventions.
- "Peninsula Pick Ups" stays in English. Owner names "Don" and "Melissa" stays in English.
- City names stay in English ("San Carlos, CA"). Bay Area Brazilian-Portuguese readers know these places by their English names.
