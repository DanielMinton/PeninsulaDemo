# Filipino (fil) — voice guide

Daly City and South San Francisco — both core Peninsula service areas — host the largest Filipino-American populations in the United States. Filipino is the standardized, Tagalog-based national language, but **code-switching with English** is the natural everyday register for Filipino-Americans, especially the 1.5- and second-generation. Translation should reflect that, not pretend English doesn't exist in the readers' speech.

## Tone

Warm, neighborly, respectful. Filipino marketing voice is generally less formal than Spanish or Russian — there's a strong cultural baseline of friendliness in service-business communication. Use **po** as the standard polite marker after key verbs and pronouns, but don't over-pepper the copy.

## Address form

- Use **kayo** (plural-formal "you") for customer-direct copy in formal contexts; **ka** (informal) is too casual for a service business addressing potential customers.
- Use **po** consistently — "Tumawag po kayo" (please call), "Salamat po" (thank you). Dropping po reads as cold.
- First-person plural for the business: **kami** (exclusive we — we, not including you) is correct for a business addressing customers. "Kami sa Peninsula Pick Ups" — "we at Peninsula Pick Ups."

## Code-switching with English

Filipino speakers in the Bay Area natively code-switch. Don't force a "pure Tagalog" translation that no one speaks — that reads as artificial. Specifically:

- Service nouns can stay English where the Filipino term is contrived: **"Junk removal"** is fine to keep partially English (e.g. "junk removal services") OR translate as **"pagtanggal ng basura"** (literally "removal of garbage"). For marketing, "junk removal" plus a Filipino verb often reads most natural.
- Technical terms (online, schedule, quote, email, SMS) typically stay English embedded in Filipino syntax.
- The brand "Peninsula Pick Ups" stays English unconditionally.

## Vocabulary

- **"Junk removal"** → **pagtanggal ng basura** is the literal translation; "junk removal" left in English is also natural. Mix as needed for rhythm.
- **"Hauling"** → **paghahakot** (the act of hauling) — strong, native-Tagalog feel.
- **"Cleanout"** → **paglilinis** (cleaning) for general; **pagsasaayos** (organizing/clearing) for property work.
- **"Pickup"** as a service action → keep brand name verbatim. For service description, **pagkuha** (collection) works.
- **"Free quote"** → **libreng quotation** or **libreng price quote** — code-switched naturally.
- **"Same-day service"** → **service sa parehong araw** (literally "service in the same day").
- **"Family-owned"** → **pag-aari ng pamilya** (owned by family). Warm.
- **"Licensed and insured"** → **may lisensya at insured** (with license and insured) — code-switched as Filipino-Americans actually speak.

## What to avoid

- **Pure Tagalog with no English mixing** in domains where code-switching is natural — sounds artificial in this register.
- **Overly formal Tagalog** (literary or pre-1972 register). Bay Area readers grew up with modern Filipino television, news, and social media — that's the level.
- **Spanish loanwords specific to formal Tagalog** that sound dated. E.g. "kustumer" is fine but "parukyano" is archaic.
- **Honorifics where unwarranted**. "Ginoo / Ginang / Binibini" reads as too formal for marketing copy. Just use "kayo" + po.

## Trust phrases (locked translations)

- "Licensed and insured" → May lisensya at insured
- "Family owned" → Pag-aari ng pamilya
- "Locally owned" → Lokal na negosyo
- "Verified business line" → Verified na business line
- "Free quote" → Libreng quotation
