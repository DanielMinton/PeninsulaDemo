# Vietnamese (vi) — voice guide

The Vietnamese-speaking community in San Mateo and Santa Clara counties is predominantly of Southern Vietnamese origin (1975-onward refugee community plus subsequent family migration). The voice should reflect Southern colloquial patterns where they don't conflict with widely-understood standard Vietnamese.

## Tone

Warm, respectful, plainspoken. Vietnamese has strong markers of register based on social distance — for a small family business, the voice should be **near**, not formal. The owners are addressed as a husband-and-wife team neighbors might know.

## Address form

Vietnamese pronoun choice signals everything. For a small business addressing potential customers:

- Refer to ourselves as **"chúng tôi"** (we, polite) — the standard business-to-customer pronoun.
- Refer to the customer as **"quý khách"** (esteemed customer) for formal contexts (form labels, legal text), or **"anh chị"** (literally older brother/sister) for warmer body copy. Avoid switching between them within the same paragraph.
- Imperatives use **"hãy"** (please) softener: "Hãy gọi cho chúng tôi" (please call us), not bare "Gọi chúng tôi."

## Vocabulary

- **"Junk removal"** → "dọn dẹp rác thải" or "thu gom đồ phế thải". The first reads as cleanout-with-haul; second as scrap collection. Use "dọn dẹp đồ phế thải" for marketing-warm phrasing.
- **"Hauling"** → "vận chuyển" is the standard; "chở" works in colloquial contexts.
- **"Cleanout"** → "dọn sạch" (clean out) or "dọn dẹp toàn bộ" (full clearance).
- **"Pickup"** as a service action → "thu gom" (collect/pick up). Brand "Peninsula Pick Ups" stays in English.
- **"Free quote"** → "báo giá miễn phí" (báo giá = quote, miễn phí = free).
- **"Same-day service"** → "dịch vụ trong ngày" (service within the day).
- **"Family-owned"** → "doanh nghiệp gia đình" (family business) — clear and warm.
- **"Licensed and insured"** → "có giấy phép và bảo hiểm đầy đủ" (with permit and full insurance).

## Diacritics — non-negotiable

Vietnamese requires full diacritics. Translations without diacritics (e.g. "lien he" instead of "liên hệ") read as foreigner-Vietnamese and are unacceptable. The translator MUST emit fully-marked Vietnamese; the QA pass should reject any output missing diacritics.

## What to avoid

- **Northern-only vocabulary** that reads as alien to Southern readers. E.g. "bố mẹ" (parents, Northern) vs "ba má" (Southern); "chè" vs "trà" for tea. For business copy, prefer pan-Vietnamese standard vocabulary; Southern colloquialisms only where they soften the register.
- **Direct English calques**. "Get a free quote" translated word-for-word as "Lấy một báo giá miễn phí" sounds clinical — better as "Nhận báo giá miễn phí" (receive a free quote) or imperative "Hãy yêu cầu báo giá miễn phí" (please request a free quote).
- **Hán-Việt over-formality**. Vietnamese has a vast Sino-Vietnamese (Hán-Việt) vocabulary that can sound bureaucratic. For marketing, prefer everyday native-Vietnamese vocabulary: "nhanh chóng" (fast) over "cấp tốc"; "đáng tin cậy" (reliable) over "tín nhiệm."
